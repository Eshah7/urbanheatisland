Concrete Traps Heat

Filtering to urban / pavement surfaces reveals the heat engine beneath the city. Parking lots, rooftops, and asphalt hold heat long after the sun sets, a phenomenon called the urban heat island effect.

Forests Are Natural Air Conditioners

02 · Vegetation & Temperature

More Green = More Cool

The NDVI (Normalized Difference Vegetation Index) measures how much living vegetation a satellite pixel contains, from bare concrete (near 0) to lush forest (near 1). The inverse relationship with temperature is stark and consistent.

~1.5°C cooler for every 0.1 increase in vegetation coverage

Each point is a 100m patch of Toronto. The red band shows the regression: a nearly 20°C spread from asphalt to forest.

03 · Land Type Comparison

The Numbers Tell the Story

When we group every satellite pixel by its land cover type, the temperature hierarchy becomes unavoidable. Urban surfaces don't just feel hotter. They are measurably, consistently hotter.

04 · Heat Equity

Who Bears the Burden?

Heat doesn't distribute randomly. Areas with less tree canopy, more pavement, and higher density run hotter, and these areas often overlap with lower-income communities that also have less access to air conditioning and cooling centres.
